# Volume of Prisms Intro Video Pack

Module: Volume of Prisms

Source: `Y6-M-L10-L18-Study Book.pdf`, Lesson 15, book pages 84-98 / PDF pages 94-108.

Audience: 11-12 year olds preparing for the Volume of Prisms practice round.

Length target: 120-150 seconds.

## Learning Outcome

After the intro, the learner should be able to see a prism as a stack of identical cross-sections, then use that idea for hollow objects, composite cross-sections, and water-displacement problems. The formula `volume = base area x height` appears only after the visual stack idea has been seen, so the rule has a clear reason rather than being a thing to memorise.

## Named Skills

The learner sees this skill card before any scene starts. Each skill maps to a registry token used by the practice bank:

- Base area - treat any prism as one cross-section stacked through a height.
- Hollow prisms - subtract the hole area from the outside area before multiplying by height.
- Composite faces - split a non-standard cross-section into rectangles and triangles, then stretch it through the prism.
- Water rise - turn a submerged volume into a water-height change, or read a water-height change as displaced volume.
- Partial submerge - when a tall block sticks out of the water, the water rises through the container area minus the block area.

## Warmup Gate

1. Cuboid volume from three dimensions.
2. Prism volume from base area and height.
3. Hollow prism volume from outside area minus hole area.
4. Composite face area from a rectangle plus a triangle, then stretched through the prism length.
5. Water displacement from base area and height change.

## Misconception Risks

- Multiplying all visible numbers even when a hole should be subtracted.
- Treating a composite cross-section as a single rectangle and missing the triangle piece.
- Using total tank height instead of the change in water height.
- Forgetting to divide by the number of equal stones in a multi-object displacement question.
- Adding the entire tall block volume when the block is only partly submerged, instead of using container area minus block area.

## Voiceover Draft

A prism is the same slice copied through a height. That single picture explains every problem in this chapter. If you can hold the stack image in your head, the formula `volume = base area x height` stops being a rule and becomes a description.

Hollow prisms add one extra step. The hole is missing space, so it never belongs in the volume. Find the outside cross-section area, find the hole cross-section area, subtract, then stretch through the prism height. Always subtract before multiplying.

Composite faces appear when the front face is not a simple rectangle. Maybe it is a rectangle plus a triangle, or an L-shape, or a square plus a trapezium. Split the front face into friendly shapes, add their areas, then stretch the combined area through the prism length. The same stack idea works.

Water rise problems use the same picture in disguise. When a stone goes fully under water, it pushes water up. The space the stone takes equals the extra water volume on top. So tank base area times water rise equals stone volume. Run the move in reverse when you know the stone volume and want the water rise: divide the stone volume by the tank base area.

Full Then Remove is a small twist. If the tank was already full while the object sat inside, taking the object out leaves a missing chunk of water. The water surface drops by that object volume divided by the tank base area.

Tall Block Trap is the partial-submergence trick. When the block sticks out above the water, only the underwater part counts as displaced volume. The water also has less floor space to fill, because the block is using some of it. So the water rise comes from the displaced volume divided by `container area minus block area`. Read that sentence twice; it is the most-missed move in this chapter.

By the end, every practice problem fits one of the five named skills: base area, hollow prisms, composite faces, water rise, or partial submerge. Name the skill before you write a number.

## NotebookLM Steering Prompt

Create a short explainer for an 11-year-old learning Volume of Prisms
from Think Academy Lesson 15. Use only the supplied lesson pages.

Start with the visual idea that a prism is the same cross-section
stacked through a height. Then introduce `volume = base area x height`.

Cover hollow prisms by subtracting the missing hole area, composite
faces by splitting the cross-section, and water-displacement problems
by connecting volume to water-level change. Include the challenge case
where a tall block is only partly submerged, so the water rises in the
container base area minus the block base area.

Keep the tone warm and concrete. End by telling the learner they will
train with fresh numbers, mixed answer formats, hints, worked
solutions, and similar repair questions after mistakes.
