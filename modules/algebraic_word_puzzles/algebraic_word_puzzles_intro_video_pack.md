# Algebraic Word Puzzles Intro Video Pack

Mission: Lesson 8 Extensive Challenges - Algebraic Word Puzzles

This intro is the launch path for the optional Extensive Challenges box on book page 144 (PDF page 154). The two source puzzles are challenge-grade for an 11-year-old: one is a hat-counting problem that hides a system of three equations behind everyday English, and the other is a pair-sums problem that hides combinatorial algebra behind a weighing scale. The intro shows a child why each puzzle yields to algebra, and names the five skills they will train.

## Core Learning Outcome

By the end of this intro, the learner can take an English statement of the form "I see ... than ..." or "the pair weighed ... kg" and rewrite it as a clean algebraic equation. They can then combine several such equations into a system, solve it by substitution, and recover a count or a weight that the original wording hid. They can also recognise when a word puzzle wants a pair-sum decomposition rather than a system, and apply the right tool.

## Named Skills Shown Before Practice

The five skills surfaced before practice, matching `registry.skills`:

- Variable substitution
- Self-exclusion counting
- System of equations
- Pair-sum decomposition
- Missing-pair logic

Each named skill maps to at least one classic in the bank and at least one storyboard scene below.

## Storyboard

The intro plays as six scenes. Each scene takes one named skill, shows the mathematical object on screen, then narrates the move in the teacher's voice.

1. **Self-Exclusion Counting**
   Open with one boy in a row of red hats. Highlight that the boy cannot see his own hat — only the other reds. The visual reduces the "boy count" by one inside the speaker's bubble. This is the trick the rest of the puzzle depends on.
   Voiceover: Start with the trick that makes every word puzzle on this page work. When somebody describes what they can see, they cannot see their own hat. So the count for their own group must be reduced by one inside that statement. Hold on to this idea while we build the rest.

2. **Variable Substitution**
   Replace the English nouns "boys / girls / teachers" with the capital letters B, G, T. The sentence "twice as many girls as teachers" rewrites to the short equation G = 2T directly on screen. The child sees the bridge from words to algebra in one stroke.
   Voiceover: Now we name what we are looking for. Let capital B be the number of boys, capital G the number of girls, capital T the number of teachers. The English sentence twice as many girls as teachers becomes the short equation G equals two T. Variable substitution is the bridge from words to algebra.

3. **System of Equations**
   Show two of the three statements as two equations stacked on a chalkboard. Animate substitution: one variable drops out, the other resolves to a whole number. The child sees a system in action with only two unknowns first.
   Voiceover: Take two of the three statements and turn each one into an equation. We now have a system of two equations in two unknowns. Substitute one equation into the other, and one variable disappears. The other variable falls out as a clean whole number.

4. **Three-Statement System**
   Add the third equation. All three speakers now contribute. Walk through the substitution chain that gives T = 6, then G = 13, then B = 16. This is the original source problem; the child should leave this scene confident they could redo it on paper.
   Voiceover: The original source problem is here. Each speaker gives one equation. The boy gives B minus G equals two. The girl gives G equals two T plus one. The teacher gives B equals T plus ten. Substitute the third into the first, then use the second, and T pops out as a whole number. From there B and G follow in one line each.

5. **Pair-Sum Decomposition**
   Switch to weights. Three people are drawn as labelled boxes A, B, C, with three lines connecting them. Each line carries a pair sum. Animate the fact that adding all three pair sums double-counts each person, so dividing by two gives S, the total weight.
   Voiceover: Switch to weights. Three people make three pair sums. Notice that every single person appears in exactly two of those three pair sums. So if you add all three pair sums together, you get twice the total of all three weights. Divide by two and you have S, the sum of every weight.

6. **Missing-Pair Logic**
   Show four people in a 2x2 grid with six pair-sum lines. One line is dashed and unlabelled — the missing weighing. Replace it with the formula `missing = 3S - total of five given`. End on the canonical numbers 47, 52, 66, 78 with 66 highlighted as the heavier of the missing pair.
   Voiceover: Four people make six pair sums. Each person now appears in exactly three pair sums. The total of all six sums equals three times the total weight S. So if only five are given, the missing sum equals three times S minus the total of the five given sums. Once you have all six sums, the smallest is the lightest pair and the largest is the heaviest pair, and the rest fall out.

## Warmup Gate

Before the full practice round opens, the learner answers three short gate questions that preview the named skills they will train:

1. A boy in a club of 10 boys says, "I see exactly 9 red hats." How many red hats does the boy NOT see, and why? (Previews Self-Exclusion Counting.)
2. Let G be the number of girls and T the number of teachers. The English sentence "there are twice as many girls as teachers" becomes which short equation? (Previews Variable Substitution.)
3. Three friends step onto a scale in pairs and get 60 kg, 70 kg, 80 kg. What is the total weight of all three friends? (Previews Pair-Sum Decomposition.)

The warmup gate matters because the source challenge problems compose all of these in one step. Without warmup mastery, the source problems read as impossible rather than puzzling.

## Misconception Risks

These are the common pitfalls an 11-year-old brings into this lesson. The intro must name each one explicitly so practice mistakes become teachable.

- Forgetting the self-exclusion trick: a child reads "I see 2 more red hats than yellow" and writes B - G = 2 instead of (B - 1) - G = 2. The intro must show, before any equation, that the speaker is invisible to themselves.
- Treating "twice as many" as "two more": a child writes G = T + 2 when the puzzle says "twice as many girls as teachers". The variable-substitution scene must contrast "twice as many" with "two more" side by side.
- Solving the pair-sum problem by trial: a child tries random four-tuples instead of using the 3S formula. The missing-pair scene must show the formula as the FIRST move, before any guessing.
- Mis-identifying smallest and largest pair: when given five sums and one missing, a child may assume the smallest given is a+b and the largest given is c+d without checking. The intro must warn that this assumption holds only when the missing pair is NOT the extreme.
- Reporting the wrong member of the missing pair: a child correctly finds the missing sum but reports the smaller person when the puzzle asks for the heavier. The final scene must make the "heavier of the missing pair" target explicit.
