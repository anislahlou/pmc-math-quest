# Triangle Sides Intro Video Pack

Mission: Lesson 17 - Relationship Between Sides In Triangles

Audience: 11-12 year olds preparing for the Triangle Sides practice round.

Length target: 100-130 seconds.

## Learning Outcome

By the end of the intro, the learner should be able to decide whether three lengths can make a triangle, then use isosceles symmetry and Pythagoras to find hidden sides, perimeters, and areas. The intro shows the visual reason for each rule before any formula appears, so a child can explain every move in one sentence.

## Named Skills

The learner sees this skill card before any scene starts. Each skill maps to a registry token used by the practice bank:

- Triangle inequality - the gate that decides whether three lengths can close into a triangle.
- Isosceles choice - which side has to repeat when only two lengths are given.
- Pythagoras - the right-triangle rule that builds or rebuilds a hypotenuse.
- Missing leg - the reverse Pythagoras move when the hypotenuse is known.
- Area chase - using shared heights and isosceles splits to find areas, perimeters, and right-turn distances.

## Storyboard

1. **Triangle Inequality**
   Visual: three coloured sticks of lengths `4`, `5`, `6` snap into a closed triangle, then a second set `2`, `2`, `4` collapses flat because the two shorter sticks cannot reach past the longest one. Duration: 12 seconds.
   Voiceover: Start with the triangle inequality. Sort the three lengths from smallest to largest. The two shorter sides must add to more than the longest side. If they only equal it, the triangle collapses flat. If they fall short, no triangle forms at all.

2. **Isosceles Choice**
   Visual: two side lengths `4` and `9` appear with a question mark on the repeated side. The child watches `4 + 4 = 8` fail past `9`, then `9 + 9 = 18` succeed past `4`. Duration: 12 seconds.
   Voiceover: An isosceles triangle has two equal sides. If a question only gives you two lengths, do not repeat the shorter one without checking. The repeated side must still let the triangle inequality close. Pick the length that passes the gate, then add for the perimeter.

3. **Pythagoras**
   Visual: a right triangle with legs `3` and `4` grows squares on each leg. Their areas, `9` and `16`, slide into a square of area `25` on the hypotenuse. Duration: 13 seconds.
   Voiceover: Pythagoras is the rule for right triangles. Square the two legs, add the squares, then take the square root. That gives the hypotenuse, the side opposite the right angle. The visual reason is that the two leg squares fit exactly inside the hypotenuse square.

4. **Missing Leg**
   Visual: a right triangle with hypotenuse `13` and one leg `5`. The hypotenuse square `169` shrinks by the leg square `25`, revealing the missing leg square `144` and leg length `12`. Duration: 12 seconds.
   Voiceover: When the hypotenuse is the known side, reverse the move. Start with the hypotenuse square, subtract the known leg square, then take the square root of what remains. That gives the missing leg. The same picture from scene three runs backwards.

5. **Area Chase**
   Visual: a big triangle is sliced by a perpendicular drop. The left smaller right triangle `5, 12, 13` is solved, then the same height `12` is reused inside the right smaller right triangle `9, 12, 15`. The total area `12 * 14 / 2 = 84` lands on screen. Duration: 13 seconds.
   Voiceover: An area chase strings several moves together. Drop one perpendicular height into a bigger triangle. Solve the first small right triangle to find that height, then carry the same height into the second small right triangle. Use Pythagoras to fill in a missing side, then turn the picture into an area or a perimeter. Name the move you are using before you write a number.

6. **Isosceles Split Area**
   Visual: an isosceles triangle with equal sides `13` and base `10` splits down the middle. The half-base `5` and equal side `13` give height `12` by Pythagoras, then base times height divided by two gives area `60`. Duration: 12 seconds.
   Voiceover: An isosceles triangle splits cleanly down the middle. The perpendicular line halves the base. That makes a right triangle whose hypotenuse is the equal side and whose short leg is half the base. Pythagoras finds the height, and area is base times height divided by two.

7. **Area To Perimeter**
   Visual: an isosceles triangle with area `48` and base `12`. The area reverses to height `8`, then Pythagoras on half-base `6` and height `8` gives equal side `10`. Perimeter pops out as `32`. Duration: 12 seconds.
   Voiceover: Sometimes the area is given and the perimeter is hidden. Double the area first, then divide by the base to find the height. Half the base and the height now sit inside a right triangle, so Pythagoras finds the equal side. Add equal, equal, base for the perimeter.

8. **Right-Turn Path**
   Visual: a path made of alternating horizontal and vertical hops `2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8`. The horizontal moves sum to `12`, the vertical moves sum to `15`. Pythagoras on `12` and `15` gives the straight-line distance. Duration: 13 seconds.
   Voiceover: A right-turn path is the same idea in disguise. Add all the sideways moves into one total. Add all the upwards moves into one total. Those two totals are the legs of one big right triangle, and the straight-line distance from start to end is the hypotenuse.

Each storyboard scene maps to one named skill in the practice bank through its `classicId`. The module test blocks release if a skill is tested but absent from the intro.

## Warmup Gate

Before full practice unlocks, the learner answers:

1. Can `4, 5, 6` form a triangle? (Yes - `4 + 5 = 9 > 6`.)
2. What is the hypotenuse if the legs are `6` and `8`? (`10` cm.)
3. What is the missing leg if the hypotenuse is `13` and the other leg is `5`? (`12` cm.)
4. In an isosceles triangle with equal sides `13` cm and base `10` cm, what is the height? (`12` cm.)

## Misconception Risks

- Adding all three sides without checking the triangle inequality first.
- Repeating the shorter side in an isosceles question even when that breaks the inequality.
- Labelling the hypotenuse as a leg because the diagram is rotated.
- Forgetting to square root after subtracting squares.
- Treating a sloping side as if it were a perpendicular height during an area chase.

## Voiceover Draft

This intro is a calm walk through eight triangle moves. The triangle inequality opens the door, because no other move matters if the three lengths cannot even close. Sort the lengths, add the two smaller, and compare with the longest. Equal does not count: the shorter pair must beat the longest side, otherwise the triangle collapses flat.

Once the gate is passed, isosceles choice handles the question style where only two lengths are given. Two equal sides must still close around the base, so the repeated side is the one that passes the inequality.

Pythagoras then arrives as the visual rule for right triangles. The two leg squares fit exactly inside the hypotenuse square, so squaring, adding, and square-rooting builds the hypotenuse. Missing leg reverses the same picture: shrink the hypotenuse square by the known leg square and read off the missing leg.

Area chase reuses one perpendicular across two right triangles inside a bigger figure. Isosceles split area uses the same idea inside one symmetrical triangle: the height splits the base in half, so Pythagoras finds the height before area divides by two.

Area to perimeter runs the area rule backwards, then uses Pythagoras to find the equal side, then adds for the perimeter. Right-turn path collapses a zigzag into one big right triangle, with all sideways moves on one leg and all upward moves on the other. By the end, every practice problem will fit into one of these named moves.
